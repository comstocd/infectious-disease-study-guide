Antimicrobial Stewardship

HIGH YIELD
üìò IDSA/SHEA Guidelines (2016, updated 2023)
  • Core elements: Leadership commitment, accountability, pharmacy expertise, action (interventions), tracking, reporting, education
  • Goal: Optimize clinical outcomes while minimizing unintended consequences (resistance, C. diff, adverse effects)
Key Stewardship Interventions
  • Prospective audit with feedback: ID/pharmacy review with recommendations (most effective)
  • Formulary restriction/preauthorization: Requires approval for restricted agents
  • IV-to-PO conversion: When patient stable, tolerating PO, GI tract functioning
  • De-escalation: Narrow spectrum based on culture data
  • Dose optimization: PK/PD-based dosing (extended infusions for Œ≤-lactams)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL
Procalcitonin-guided therapy: Can safely reduce antibiotic duration in respiratory infections and sepsis. PCT <0.25 ng/mL suggests low likelihood of bacterial infection; PCT <0.5 or 80% decrease from peak supports stopping antibiotics.

üíé CLINICAL PEARL
Extended infusion piperacillin-tazobactam: 4-hour infusion (vs 30-min) improves fT>MIC, shown to reduce mortality in critically ill patients with Pseudomonas infections (BLING III trial, Lancet 2022).
Vancomycin Dosing (IDSA 2020)
  • Target: AUC/MIC 400-600 (replaces trough-based dosing)
  • Loading dose: 25-30 mg/kg (actual body weight) for serious infections
  • Maintenance: 15-20 mg/kg q8-12h, adjust based on AUC
  • AUC monitoring preferred: Two-level (Bayesian) approach most accurate
  • Nephrotoxicity risk: AUC >600 or trough >20 increases risk

Culture Interpretation & Common Contaminants

HIGH YIELD
Blood Culture Interpretation
  • Always pathogens: S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, Œ≤-hemolytic strep, Enterobacterales, Pseudomonas, Candida, Listeria
  • Usually contaminants: Coagulase-negative staph (CoNS), Corynebacterium, Bacillus (non-anthracis), Propionibacterium
  • CoNS interpretation: Likely pathogen if ‚â•2 positive bottles, same species, patient with intravascular device, or immunocompromised
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: Post-op patient with fever, 1 of 2 blood cultures positive for coagulase-negative staphylococcus at 36 hours.

Answer: Most likely contaminant. Do not treat. Repeat cultures if clinically concerned. True CoNS bacteremia typically has ‚â•2 positive cultures with identical species.

Time to Positivity (TTP)
  • Earlier positivity (<12-14 hours) suggests higher bacterial burden
  • S. aureus bacteremia: TTP <12h associated with endocarditis, metastatic infection
  • Contaminants often have longer TTP (>24-48 hours)

HIGH YIELD
Spectrum
Gram-positive ONLY MRSA

  • Coagulase-negative staph

Aminoglycosides

CORE
Key Agents
  • Gentamicin: Synergy for enterococcal/streptococcal endocarditis
  • Tobramycin: Best anti-pseudomonal; inhaled for CF
  • Amikacin: Broadest; often active against gentamicin-resistant organisms

  • Neuromuscular blockade: Rare; risk in myasthenia gravis

Other Important Antibiotics

HIGH YIELD
Daptomycin
  • Spectrum: Gram-positive only; excellent MRSA, VRE activity
  • Mechanism: Calcium-dependent membrane depolarization
  • Key uses: MRSA bacteremia, right-sided endocarditis, complicated SSTI
  • Contraindicated in pneumonia: Inactivated by pulmonary surfactant
  • Toxicity: CPK elevation (check weekly); eosinophilic pneumonia
  • Dosing: 6 mg/kg for bacteremia; 8-10 mg/kg may improve outcomes
Linezolid
  • Spectrum: Gram-positive including MRSA, VRE
  • Mechanism: Oxazolidinone; inhibits 50S ribosome initiation
  • Key uses: MRSA pneumonia (penetrates lung well), VRE infections
  • 100% oral bioavailability: IV = PO dosing
  • Toxicity: Myelosuppression (thrombocytopenia > anemia), peripheral/optic neuropathy, serotonin syndrome with SSRIs, lactic acidosis
  • Duration limit: Use <2 weeks if possible (myelosuppression)
Metronidazole
  • Spectrum: Anaerobes (NOT aerobic bacteria), protozoa
  • Key uses: C. difficile, intra-abdominal infections, bacterial vaginosis, trichomoniasis, amebiasis, giardiasis
  • Excellent oral bioavailability
  • Toxicity: Disulfiram reaction with alcohol, metallic taste, peripheral neuropathy (prolonged use)
  • CNS penetration: Excellent; used for brain abscesses (anaerobic component)
Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX)
  • Spectrum: CA-MRSA, PCP, Nocardia, Stenotrophomonas, Listeria
  • Key uses: PCP prophylaxis/treatment, CA-MRSA SSTI, UTI
  • Toxicity: Hyperkalemia, myelosuppression (especially with methotrexate), rash/SJS, crystalluria
  • Drug interactions: Warfarin (‚Üë INR), methotrexate toxicity, ACE-I (hyperkalemia)
Doxycycline
  • Spectrum: Atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella), MRSA, Rickettsia, spirochetes
  • Key uses: CAP (atypical coverage), MRSA SSTI, STIs, Lyme disease, RMSF, malaria prophylaxis
  • Safe in renal failure: No adjustment needed
  • Toxicity: Photosensitivity, esophageal ulceration (take with water, upright), teeth discoloration (avoid in children <8)

HIGH YIELD

HIGH YIELD

  • VAP: Pneumonia occurring >48 hours after endotracheal intubation
üìò ATS/IDSA HAP/VAP Guidelines (2016)

  • Septic shock at time of VAP
  • ARDS preceding VAP
  • ‚â•5 days hospitalization prior to VAP
  • Acute renal replacement therapy prior to VAP
  • Most important: Local antibiogram data (>10-20% resistance = add coverage)

  • Gram-negative: Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Klebsiella, E. coli, Enterobacter, Acinetobacter
  • MRSA: Especially if prior colonization, hemodialysis, prior antibiotics
  • Polymicrobial: Common

Aspiration Pneumonia & Lung Abscess

HIGH YIELD
Key Concepts
  • Aspiration pneumonitis: Chemical injury from gastric acid; does NOT require antibiotics unless secondary infection develops
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Infection from aspirated oropharyngeal bacteria; requires antibiotics
Aspiration Pneumonia
  • Risk factors: Dysphagia, altered consciousness, GERD, NG tube, poor dentition
  • Pathogens: Streptococci, anaerobes (if poor dentition/periodontal disease), gram-negatives (if hospitalized)
  • Location: Right lower lobe (when upright), posterior segments of upper lobes (when supine)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Anaerobic Coverage
Anaerobic coverage NOT routinely needed for aspiration pneumonia unless:
  • Severe periodontal disease
  • Putrid sputum
  • Lung abscess or empyema present
  • Evidence of necrotizing pneumonia
If needed: Ampicillin-sulbactam, amoxicillin-clavulanate, or add metronidazole
Lung Abscess
  • Etiology: Aspiration (most common), post-obstruction (cancer), hematogenous (Staph, septic emboli)
  • Microbiology: Polymicrobial; anaerobes + streptococci; consider MRSA if risk factors
  • Treatment: Antibiotics (ampicillin-sulbactam, clindamycin, or metronidazole + Œ≤-lactam)
  • Duration: Until cavity resolves or minimal stable residual (often 4-8 weeks)
  • Drainage: Consider if >6 cm, not responding to antibiotics, or empyema
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: 62-year-old alcoholic with poor dentition presents with 3 weeks of fever, productive cough with foul-smelling sputum. CXR shows cavitary lesion in right lower lobe.

Diagnosis: Lung abscess (aspiration, anaerobes)

Treatment: Ampicillin-sulbactam or clindamycin √ó weeks until cavity resolves

Encephalitis

HIGH YIELD
Key Features Distinguishing from Meningitis
  • Altered mental status/personality changes ‚Äî hallmark
  • Seizures
  • Focal neurologic deficits
  • Movement disorders
  • Meningismus may be present or absent
Etiology
  • HSV-1: Most common cause of sporadic encephalitis (treat empirically!)
  • Arboviruses: West Nile, Eastern equine, La Crosse (seasonal, summer-fall)
  • VZV: Especially immunocompromised
  • Autoimmune: Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis (young women, ovarian teratoma)
üö® HSV ENCEPHALITIS
  • Start acyclovir empirically ‚Äî do not wait for PCR results
  • MRI: Temporal lobe involvement (T2/FLAIR hyperintensity)
  • EEG: Periodic lateralizing epileptiform discharges (PLEDs)
  • CSF HSV PCR: May be negative early; repeat if high suspicion
  • Treatment: IV acyclovir 10 mg/kg q8h √ó 14-21 days
  • Mortality without treatment: 70%
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE: HSV Encephalitis

Scenario: 35-year-old with 3 days of fever, headache, confusion, and new-onset seizures. MRI shows bilateral temporal lobe enhancement. CSF shows lymphocytic pleocytosis.

Answer: HSV encephalitis. Start IV acyclovir immediately. Send CSF HSV PCR.

üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE: West Nile

Scenario: 72-year-old in late summer with fever, weakness, flaccid paralysis, tremor. CSF lymphocytic pleocytosis.

Answer: West Nile virus neuroinvasive disease. Polio-like flaccid paralysis is characteristic. Diagnosis: serum/CSF WNV IgM. No specific treatment.

Brain Abscess

HIGH YIELD
Etiology & Sources
  • Contiguous spread (40-50%): Sinusitis, otitis, dental infection ‚Üí frontal/temporal lobe
  • Hematogenous (25%): Endocarditis, pulmonary AVMs, lung abscess ‚Üí multiple abscesses
  • Trauma/neurosurgery: S. aureus predominant
  • Cryptogenic (25%): No source identified
Microbiology
  • Polymicrobial common (30-60%)
  • Streptococci: Viridans group, S. anginosus (milleri) group
  • Anaerobes: Bacteroides, Fusobacterium, Prevotella
  • S. aureus: Trauma, neurosurgery, endocarditis
  • Gram-negatives: Otogenic source, immunocompromised
  • Immunocompromised: Nocardia, Toxoplasma, Aspergillus, Cryptococcus
Treatment
  • Empiric antibiotics: Ceftriaxone + metronidazole (¬± vancomycin if MRSA risk)
  • Duration: 6-8 weeks IV
  • Surgical drainage: Abscesses >2.5 cm, mass effect, no improvement with antibiotics
  • Steroids: For significant mass effect (controversial)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL
LP is contraindicated in brain abscess due to risk of herniation. Diagnosis is made by neuroimaging (MRI with contrast: ring-enhancing lesion with restricted diffusion).

Malaria

HIGH YIELD
üö® MEDICAL EMERGENCY
Consider malaria in ANY febrile patient with travel to endemic area. P. falciparum can be fatal within 24-48 hours.
Species & Clinical Features
  • P. falciparum: Most severe; cerebral malaria, severe anemia, ARDS, hypoglycemia, renal failure
  • P. vivax/P. ovale: Hypnozoite stage (liver) ‚Üí relapses; need primaquine for radical cure
  • P. malariae: Chronic infection; nephrotic syndrome
Diagnosis
  • Thick smear: Screening (higher sensitivity)
  • Thin smear: Species identification, parasitemia quantification
  • Rapid diagnostic test: Antigen detection; good for non-endemic areas
Treatment
  • Uncomplicated (chloroquine-sensitive): Chloroquine
  • Uncomplicated (chloroquine-resistant, most areas): Artemether-lumefantrine (Coartem) OR atovaquone-proguanil (Malarone)
  • Severe malaria: IV artesunate (first-line); exchange transfusion if >10% parasitemia
  • P. vivax/ovale: Add primaquine (after checking G6PD status) to prevent relapse
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Chloroquine Resistance
Assume chloroquine resistance for P. falciparum from sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia. Only Central America (west of Panama Canal), Caribbean, and Middle East still have chloroquine-sensitive P. falciparum.

Strongyloides

HIGH YIELD
üö® HYPERINFECTION SYNDROME
Life-threatening in immunocompromised (especially steroids). Screen and treat BEFORE immunosuppression in patients from endemic areas.

  • Serology: More sensitive but may cross-react with other helminths

  • BSI in patient with central line in place within 48 hours
